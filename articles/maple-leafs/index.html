The Great Possession Lie

Eighteen seasons of Toronto Maple Leafs 5-on-5 data reveal a team that has always found creative ways to look better — or worse — than it actually is.

By Ryan Ghizzoni · March 2026

Goals For percentage (GF%) is simple: of all goals scored while a team is at 5-on-5, what share did they score? Above 50% means you outscored the opponent; below 50% means you didn't. But in hockey, the team that generates more shots and more dangerous chances tends to win over time. A team can run a positive GF% for a season on hot goaltending and a shooting percentage spike. It cannot do so forever.

The Maple Leafs have spent nearly two decades demonstrating both sides of that truth. The data below covers every regular season from 2007–08 through the current 2025–26 campaign, all strength-adjusted at 5-on-5. What emerges is a portrait of a franchise that traded in illusions long before it learned to build something real — and may be sliding back toward old habits.

01

Here are nineteen seasons of Toronto Maple Leafs hockey, measured by Goals For percentage at 5-on-5 — the share of goals scored while teams were at even strength.

The dotted line at 50% separates teams that outscored their opponents from those that didn't. The shape of this curve tells a story of two very different franchises wearing the same jersey.

02

From 2008 through 2016, the Leafs posted a GF% above 50% exactly once — in the lockout-shortened 2012–13 season. Every other year they were net losers at even strength.

Through this stretch the team cycled through coaches and GMs but the underlying math barely budged. They were outshot, out-chanced, and outscored at 5-on-5, year after year.

03

That 2012–13 spike deserves scrutiny. The Leafs posted a 52.2% GF% — but their Corsi For percentage (shot attempts) was just 44.9%, the worst in the dataset.

They won the goal battle by running a 10.5% shooting percentage, among the highest of the era. The underlying process was a disaster. That spring, they led Boston 3–1 in the first round of the playoffs before losing four straight. The regression was swift and brutal.

04

The bill arrived in 2013–14. With shooting luck normalized, the Leafs' GF% fell back to 47.0% — but the possession numbers cratered further. Their CF% hit 42.4%, the worst single-season mark in this entire dataset.

They allowed 67.2 shot attempts against per 60 minutes while generating just 49.4 for. The gap was so severe that management finally acknowledged the roster was not a playoff-caliber team dressed up as one.

05

The tank culminated in 2015–16. GF% bottomed out at 42.6% — the lowest in the modern Leafs era — but this time the cause was different.

Corsi was actually even (CF% 50.3%), but the team's shooting percentage collapsed to 6.4%, the lowest in the dataset. They were creating chances and not converting. It earned them the first overall pick and the right to draft Auston Matthews.

06

Matthews changed everything immediately. Over the three seasons from 2016–17 through 2018–19, the Leafs posted their first sustained run of positive GF% since the data begins.

More importantly, this wasn't shooting-luck driven. CF% held near 51–52%, and xGF% — the model-based expected goals share — tracked above 51% for the first time. The process and the results aligned. The rebuild had worked.

07

The three seasons from 2020–21 through 2022–23 represent peak Maple Leafs by this measure. GF% averaged 56.2% across that stretch. Expected goals percentage averaged 55.3% — meaning the dominance was real, not a fluke.

The 2020–21 bubble season stands alone at 58.2% GF%, a figure inflated by the unusual format. But 2021–22 and 2022–23 were legitimate: xGF% of 56.1% and 54.1% respectively. This team, at even strength, was genuinely elite.

08

The 2024–25 season looked fine on the surface: GF% 55.9%, one of the best marks in franchise history. But beneath it, a familiar story was forming.

CF% fell to 47.7% — below even — while xGF% dropped to 49.4%. The Leafs were being outchanced but converting at a 9.4% clip. The gap between what they deserved and what they got was the widest it had been since the dark ages. History suggests that gap closes — painfully.

09

Through 65 games in 2025–26, the gap has closed. GF% sits at 45.9% — the worst since the 2015–16 tank — with a GA/60 of 3.06 goals against per 60 minutes at even strength, the highest figure in this entire dataset.

CF% is 44.9% and xGF% is 47.3%. This is not bad luck. The underlying process has deteriorated to levels not seen since the pre-Matthews era. Whether this represents a genuine structural collapse or a correctable mid-season crisis is the most important question in Toronto hockey right now.

The through-line across all nineteen seasons is that GF% alone is not enough. The 2012–13 Leafs posted 52.2% on an unsustainable process. The 2024–25 Leafs posted 55.9% the same way. Both times, the underlying Corsi and expected-goals numbers were flashing red. Both times, the team's shooting percentage masked it — until it didn't.

What the Matthews era did, at its peak, was produce both: a team that outscored opponents and outchanced them. That combination, from 2021 through 2024, was genuine. What 2025–26 suggests is that the roster construction around that core has eroded. The Leafs are back to surviving on percentages, and with 17 games remaining in the regular season, survival is the right word.

Data: Evolving Hockey standard team statistics at 5-on-5, regular season, score and venue adjusted. All percentages are share-of-total (i.e., TOR ÷ (TOR + OPP)). The 2012–13 season was shortened by the CBA lockout to 48 games; the 2019–20 season was shortened by COVID-19 to 70 games; the 2020–21 season was played in a bubble format of 56 games.
